Yet when we move from **single prompts** to **real deployments**—long-lived projects, multiple tools, many stakeholders—typical AI systems begin to fail in predictable ways:


<!-- Page 2 of 10 -->


- They lose track of important context over time. - They contradict earlier decisions because the “why” is gone. - They fill gaps with confident hallucinations instead of admitting ignorance. - They treat human oversight as an afterthought rather than a design principle.  The RCT ecosystem does **not** start from the question:  > “What can this model do if we prompt it nicely?”  Instead, it starts from:  > “**Why do current AI systems fail in practice?**  > and what kind of architecture, memory, and language would be required to fix that?”  This chapter:  1. Defines **eight large AI problems** observed in real usage. 2. Highlights the **Context Window Crisis** as the most visible symptom. 3. Prepares the ground for how **FDIA**, **JITNA**, **Vault-1068**, **RCTDB**,  and the **9-tier / 36-algorithm** stack are designed as a *coherent response*.  ---  ## 1.2 From Single-Prompt AI to Ecosystem-Level AI  We can think of current AI usage along three layers:  1. **Prompt-level usage**  - One user, one model, one prompt.  - “Chat with AI”, “Generate a code snippet”, “Summarize a file”.  2. **App-level usage**  - AI embedded inside a single product or feature.  - Some retrieval (RAG), some history, limited scope, one domain at a time.  3. **Ecosystem-level usage**  - Many domains, long-running workflows, multiple agents, multiple tools.  - Needs shared memory, governance, versioning, verification, and economic constraints.  The **RCT ecosystem is explicitly designed for layer 3**.  To justify RCT’s design, we must first understand **what breaks** when we try to scale from layer 1–2 to layer 3. The next sections name these failure modes as the “8 Big AI Problems”.  ---

## 1.3 The 8 Big AI Problems  ### Problem 1 — Context Window & Fragmented Memory  Most LLMs operate with a finite context window. Even when the raw token limit is large, we encounter:  - **Fragmented knowledge** scattered across PDFs, wikis, chats, and code.  - **Lossy compression** when we over-summarize past work.  - **Temporal drift** when the current context no longer matches what drove earlier decisions.  In practice this means:  - The model answers from a *local slice* of knowledge, not the *global picture*.  - Different parts of a project contradict each other.  - Teams repeat decisions because the system cannot remember *why* something was done.  **RCT view:**  The **Context Window Crisis** is not merely “too few tokens”. It is the absence of a designed memory architecture and language:  - **Vault-1068** provides a curated, structured knowledge backbone.  - **RCTDB** stores experiments, deltas, and decision histories.  - **FDIA + JITNA** give us a way to *address* knowledge as roles (I/D/Δ/A/R/M),  rather than hoping the window is big enough.  ---  ### Problem 2 — Lossy Retrieval & Hallucinated Gaps  Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) is often used as a patch for context limits. In practice:  - Retrieval is frequently **ad-hoc** (simple keyword or naive vector search).  - There is little notion of *importance*, *role*, or *trust level* of a document.  - When retrieval misses something critical, the model tends to **hallucinate** smoothly.  The dangerous part:  > The model’s tone of certainty is nearly identical whether it is grounded or hallucinating.  **RCT view:**  Knowledge organization and retrieval are promoted to **first-class design**:  - Vault-1068 and RCTDB are tagged by FDIA/JITNA roles and tiers,  not treated as a flat document pile.  - The system is designed to **know when it does not know**,  and to surface uncertainty as part of its behavior.

<!-- Page 4 of 10 -->


- Experiments recorded in RCTDB explicitly test “hallucination under retrieval failure”  as a recurring benchmark scenario.  ---  ### Problem 3 — Intent Misalignment & Prompt Fragility  Typical AI systems are extremely sensitive to:  - subtle changes in wording,  - ordering of instructions,  - and hidden assumptions in the user’s mental model.  The core issue is **weak intent modeling**:  - Prompts are treated as the full definition of intent.  - There is no persistent representation of “what the user is *really* trying to achieve”  over weeks or across projects.  - Intent does not survive across tools, workflows, or agents.  Consequences:  - Outputs are unstable across runs.  - Long-term workflows are fragile.  - “Prompt engineering” becomes trial-and-error craft instead of a structured language.  **RCT view:**  - **JITNA** formalizes intent as a six-field packet:  `I, D, Δ, A, R, M`.  - **FDIA** elevates **Intent (I)** to an exponent:  improving I changes F *non-linearly* even when D stays constant.  - In RCT, the raw prompt string is just *one surface form* of intent;  the true unit is the JITNA packet, stored and evolving over time.  ---  ### Problem 4 — Opaque Reasoning & Non-verifiable Chains  Most LLM reasoning is opaque:  - We see input and output; the chain in between is hidden or only loosely exposed.  - “Chain-of-thought” text can itself be hallucinated and is rarely replayable.  - There is usually no tight connection between reasoning and persistent artifacts.  For serious work (engineering, legal, research, policy):  - We need to **trace** how conclusions were reached.


<!-- Page 5 of 10 -->


- We need to **replay** decisions under new data or rules.  - We need to **check** that critical constraints were respected, not merely hoped for.  **RCT view:**  - Verification is part of the architecture, not an add-on.  - Algorithms are designed to check and cross-check each other.  - **SignedAI-style verification layers** and experiment logs in **RCTDB**  turn one-off model runs into **auditable, replayable artifacts**.  - FDIA/JITNA states are written into Vault-1068 & RCTDB alongside outputs,  so we can ask later *why* a result was produced.  ---  ### Problem 5 — Static Architectures in Dynamic Tasks  Many AI apps are built as **rigid pipelines**:  - Always call `model X` with `prompt Y`,  - or always run `A → B → C` regardless of context.  Real workloads are not static:  - Some tasks need a quick, shallow pass.  - Others demand multi-step planning, multi-agent debate, and deep verification.  - Constraints such as cost, latency, and risk change over time.  **RCT view:**  - RCT defines **36 algorithms across 9 tiers**, each with a specific role.  - A **meta-architecture** chooses different routes depending on the JITNA state:  - small Δ + low risk → fast path, fewer tiers.  - large Δ + high stakes → deep path with verification and SignedAI.  - This meta-architecture is encoded in the vault and orchestrators,  so new models and tools can be swapped in **without rebuilding the whole system**.  ---  ### Problem 6 — Tool & Workflow Fragmentation  Most organizations end up with:  - multiple vendors,  - multiple internal AI tools,  - and scattered scripts & workflows with their own prompts and assumptions.  Effects:


<!-- Page 6 of 10 -->


- Knowledge is siloed per tool.  - There is no global memory or global intent representation.  - Routing tasks to “the best tool for this context” is manual and inconsistent.  **RCT view:**  - RCT treats the ecosystem as a **governed marketplace of tools and models**,  orchestrated by FDIA and JITNA.  - A shared memory layer (Vault-1068 + RCTDB) underpins all tools.  - Tool choice, ordering, and constraints (cost, latency, risk) are encoded as part of  JITNA’sA/Δ/R components and stored for later analysis.  ---  ### Problem 7 — Evaluation & Verification Mismatch  Common benchmarks:  - are narrow and synthetic,  - rarely match the tasks a real team cares about,  - and are run once, not treated as living assets.  In production:  - Systems fail in ways benchmarks never measured.  - There is no **closed loop** from failure → new test → improved algorithm.  **RCT view:**  - Evaluation is **built into** the product, not separate.  - RCT uses:  - hundreds of test scenarios and  - thousands of experiments logged in RCTDB  as a continuously growing testbed.  - When a real failure is observed, it becomes a new test.  - Algorithms must pass the accumulated test history, with FDIA/JITNA state recorded,  before being promoted.  ---  ### Problem 8 — Human-in-the-Loop as Afterthought  In many systems:  - Humans are brought in “only when something goes wrong”.  - There is no explicit model of:  - when humans should decide,  - what they are uniquely good at,


<!-- Page 7 of 10 -->


- and how their feedback should persist.  Consequences:  - Oversight is inconsistent and exhausting.  - Human judgment is not recorded as a first-class artifact.  - Long-term commitments and ethical decisions are fragile.  **RCT view:**  - The **Architect Model** puts humans (and their codices, genomes, and principles)  at the top of the hierarchy.  - FDIA fixes **A = Architect**, not Action.  - JITNA’s **R and M** fields encode reflection and memory, so human decisions and  corrections become part of the system’s long-term behavior.  - Oversight is **designed into** the architecture, not bolted on later.  ---  ## 1.4 The Context Window Crisis  ### 1.4.1 Beyond “Too Few Tokens”  Simply increasing context length (8k → 32k → 128k → 1M tokens) helps some cases but leaves three deeper problems unsolved:  1. **Storage** — what to keep, compress, or discard.  2. **Addressing** — how to find the right knowledge at the right time.  3. **Temporal reasoning** — how to reason about what was known *when*.  Unstructured, ever-larger contexts:  - increase compute cost and latency,  - do not guarantee better reasoning,  - and can degrade performance when irrelevant details drown out key signals.  ### 1.4.2 Three Layers of the Crisis  1. **Storage crisis**  - The world’s knowledge does not fit into any single window.  - We need principled retention, compression, and summarization.  2. **Addressing crisis**  - We must query knowledge by **role and semantics**, not just by text similarity.  - Example: “Show all decisions with high Δ that were later revised, and why.”  3. **Temporal crisis**  - Context evolves. Rules change. New experiments invalidate old assumptions.


<!-- Page 8 of 10 -->


- The system needs a memory of “governance over time”, not just snapshots.  ### 1.4.3 RCT’s Response  RCT addresses these layers via coordinated design:  - **Vault-1068**  - A structured, versioned knowledge vault, curated for machine + human reading.  - Each file tagged with FDIA/JITNA roles, tiers, domains.  - The **manifest-1068** acts as a map across the ecosystem.  - **RCTDB**  - A database layer for:  - long-term memory,  - experiment logs,  - deltas between earlier and improved designs.  - Enables queries like:  - “Which experiment led us to adopt this architecture?”  - “What were the outcomes before vs after a codex change?”  - **FDIA & JITNA**  - Provide a **semantic coordinate system**:  - **I** for what we are trying to achieve.  - **D** for what is currently known.  - **Δ** for how far we are from the goal.  - **A, R, M** for how we act, reflect, and remember.  - Context windows become **views** into this larger structure  rather than the structure itself.  ---  ## 1.5 Why Local Fixes Are Not Enough  Common “fixes” today:  - bigger models, longer context windows,  - point-solution RAG for one app,  - prompt libraries, ad-hoc guardrails,  - manual checklists outside the AI system,  bring incremental gains but do not:  - unify intent across tools,  - unify memory across time,  - embed governance and human judgment into the core architecture.  RCT’s diagnosis is that the current landscape suffers from **architectural debt**:


<!-- Page 9 of 10 -->


> The components exist — models, tools, logging, retrieval —  > but they are not arranged under a single design language and intent.  FDIA, JITNA, Vault-1068, RCTDB, the 36 algorithms, and 22→33 containers,  plus the three domains (RCTLabs, ArtentAI, SignedAI) are designed as **one answer** to this debt.  ---  ## 1.6 From Problem Space to RCT Design  This chapter can be read as a **requirements document** for the rest of the whitepaper:  - Because of **Problems 1 & 2 (Context & Retrieval)**  → RCT must provide Vault-1068, RCTDB, and principled compression & querying.  - Because of **Problem 3 (Intent Fragility)**  → RCT must introduce JITNA and treat **I** as a first-class driver in FDIA.  - Because of **Problems 4 & 7 (Opaque Reasoning & Verification)**  → RCT must embed verification, logging, and replay into algorithms and infra.  - Because of **Problems 5 & 6 (Static Architectures & Fragmentation)**  → RCT must define a modular meta-architecture (36 algorithms / 9 tiers)  plus a routing layer governed by FDIA/JITNA.  - Because of **Problem 8 (Human Afterthought)**  → RCT must encode the Architect’s role, codices, and governance directly  into the system (genomes, constitutions, oversight flows).  Later chapters should therefore be read not as isolated features,  but as **structured answers to the eight problems** defined here.  ---  ## 1.7 Summary  In this chapter we:  1.
